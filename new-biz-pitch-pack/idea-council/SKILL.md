# Idea Council

Takes a selected differentiator from a client research brief and runs it through six creative pressure-test prompts, each generating a distinct, named marketing approach. Ends with a primary and secondary recommendation, then packages the winning approach for the Pitch Package skill.

---

## Step 0: Intake

Read the handoff payload from the client-research-brief skill. If no payload is present, ask the user to paste it or run the research brief first.

Present the three differentiators clearly:

> **Which differentiator do you want to run through the council?**
>
> **1.** [Unique Differentiator 1 — one sentence]
> **2.** [Unique Differentiator 2 — one sentence]
> **3.** [Unique Differentiator 3 — one sentence]

Wait for the user to select one before proceeding. Carry the full payload context (category snapshot summary, customer pressure-test summary, and selected differentiator with its Challenge and Value) into all six prompts.

---

## Output Standard

Before running the prompts, internalize this standard. Every approach generated by the six prompts must follow this exact format:

---

**[Approach Name]** — A 3-5 word title that captures the creative angle. Should feel like a campaign name, not a description.

**The Angle:** One sentence explaining the strategic logic — why this approach works given what we know about the customer and the differentiator.

**The Execution:** 2-3 sentences describing what this looks like in practice. Specific enough that a writer or strategist could pick it up and run. Could be a campaign concept, a content strategy, a positioning statement, a pitch hook, or a channel approach — whatever fits the angle best.

---

Approaches should be genuinely distinct from each other. If two approaches feel like variations of the same idea, push one further until it's not.

---

## The Six Prompts

Run all six in sequence. Use the selected differentiator, its Challenge and Value, and the category and customer context from the payload to inform every response. Adapt each prompt from personal ideation framing to client/differentiator framing.

---

### Prompt 1: The Non-Obvious Bold Step

**Strategic logic:** What's the uncomfortable move that most brands in this category would never make — but that this differentiator uniquely enables?

Ask: *What unconventional or non-obvious marketing decision could this brand make today that would change the trajectory of how this differentiator lands in the market?*

The approach should feel slightly uncomfortable but directionally correct. Avoid the obvious move — the thing every competitor is already doing.

---

### Prompt 2: The Objection Flip

**Strategic logic:** Surface the sharpest customer objection to this differentiator, then make that objection the centerpiece of the marketing.

Ask: *What are the top fears, objections, or unknowns a skeptical ideal customer would have about this differentiator before buying? Which objection is so common and so real that leading with it — and answering it — would build more trust than any benefit claim?*

The approach should be built around the most powerful objection, not around avoiding it.

---

### Prompt 3: The Blind Spot Reveal

**Strategic logic:** Find the assumption baked into the brand's current positioning that customers have quietly stopped believing — and build an approach around telling the truth instead.

Ask: *What is this brand or category not saying that customers already know? What uncomfortable truth, if acknowledged openly, would make this differentiator feel more credible than any polished claim?*

The approach should feel like the brand is on the customer's side, not selling to them.

---

### Prompt 4: The 10x Agency Play

**Strategic logic:** Temporarily ignore feasibility and budget constraints. If this brand had complete freedom to express this differentiator at full scale, what would they do?

Ask: *If constraints didn't exist — budget, channel, format, convention — what would the most ambitious version of marketing this differentiator look like? What would they build, say, or do that no brand in this category has ever tried?*

The approach should be bold enough to feel slightly unrealistic, but grounded enough in the differentiator that it's directionally useful even if scaled back.

---

### Prompt 5: The Daily Pain Anchor

**Strategic logic:** Find the specific, recurring daily moment where the customer feels the absence of this differentiator most acutely — and make that moment the entire campaign.

Ask: *What is the most specific, relatable daily frustration this differentiator eliminates? Not the category problem — the exact moment, the exact feeling, the exact Tuesday at 6:47pm that this product solves.*

The approach should be rooted in a scene so recognizable that the target customer reads it and thinks "that's me."

---

### Prompt 6: The Smallest True Thing

**Strategic logic:** Strip away every claim, benefit, and feature until only the most irreducible, undeniable truth about this differentiator remains — then build the approach around that single truth.

Ask: *What is the one thing about this differentiator that is so obviously true, so simply stated, that it needs no explanation or proof? What's the version of this message that a customer could repeat to a friend in one sentence?*

The approach should feel like clarity, not cleverness. The best version of this prompt produces something that sounds like it's been true all along.

---

## Council Recommendation

After all six approaches, write a brief council summary:

**Primary Recommendation:** Name the approach and explain in 2-3 sentences why it's the strongest — what it gets right about the customer, the differentiator, and the competitive landscape.

**Secondary Recommendation:** Name the approach and explain in 2-3 sentences why it's the backup — often a safer execution of a similar insight, or a strong complement to the primary.

**Why the others didn't win:** One sentence each on the remaining four — not dismissive, just honest about why they ranked lower for this specific brief.

Then ask:

> **Which approach do you want to take forward to the Pitch Package?**
> You can go with my primary recommendation, the secondary, or any of the six. Just name it and I'll package it up.

## Tone & Style Guidelines

- Write each approach as if briefing a creative director — specific, confident, and free of hedging
- Approach names should feel like campaign titles, not feature descriptions
- The six approaches should collectively feel like a menu of genuinely different strategic bets, not variations on a theme
- The council recommendation should be direct — say clearly which one you'd go with and why
- Never pad — if an approach is weaker, say so briefly and move on
